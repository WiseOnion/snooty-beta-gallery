The Problem

How beauty professionals work today

Booking and running a client business today means stitching together tools that were never built for it:
• Instagram DMs for booking requests
• Text messages for confirmations and reminders
• A notes app for tracking client history
• Cash App for deposits, with no enforcement if a client backs out
• Square for the appointment and the charge itself

The real workflow, for many stylists, looks like this: someone finds your work on Instagram or TikTok, slides into your DMs, asks "how much for this," negotiates a price and date over text, and hopes the deposit gets mentioned before appointment day. No calendar, no guaranteed slot, no enforced policy on either side.
That's not a hypothetical: industry data puts no-show and last-minute-cancellation rates for beauty appointments at 10–20% when nothing enforces a deposit.

The result: constant back-and-forth questions, deposits that have to be manually chased, cancellations with no real policy behind them, double bookings from juggling several tools at once, and real time lost every single day to admin that isn't cutting hair.

We're not showing you a fix yet. We're naming the problem first.

Why Existing Platforms Failed

Why StyleSeat, Booksy, Fresha, and Vagaro don't solve this

These platforms exist and plenty of stylists already use one. They still don't solve the actual problem:
• Built around a calendar and a booking widget, not around getting found by someone new.
• A client still has to already know your name or handle to search for you on them — they manage existing demand, they don't generate new demand.
• Their real competitor is Instagram, and Instagram already wins on discovery. None of them solve the operational side either: scheduling, enforced deposits, no-show protection, or reviews tied to a real completed appointment instead of just a star count.
• Pricing: StyleSeat $35/mo flat plus a 20% new-client fee (a recurring public complaint). Booksy $29.99/mo plus a "Boost" feature that takes 30% of first-time bookings. Fresha's core plan is free but takes 20% on marketplace-driven new clients, with a $20/mo fee reportedly rolling out. Vagaro starts at $23.99/mo plus $10 per extra calendar. Most bill every month whether or not a stylist gets booked that month.
• Wrong incentive: several of these get paid on a subscription regardless of whether the stylist actually earns anything that month.

Our Philosophy

Clients don't search for stylists. They search for looks.

Snooty starts with inspiration, not a directory. A client may not know exactly what style she wants, but she knows she wants to leave looking and feeling better than she came in.
Why this matters more here than in most beauty categories: these are often multi-hour, higher-cost appointments where trust in the specific stylist's actual work matters more than almost anywhere else — a client isn't picking a stylist off a generic list, she's evaluating real finished photos before committing hours and real money.
Everything else in the product follows from that one idea: search by hairstyle instead of by name, a profile that proves the work is real once she's interested, a booking flow that turns interest into a committed appointment, and a business toolkit that runs itself once she's booked.

Discover is the client's home screen. It's where people in your local area browse real finished looks, search for services like "balayage," "layered cut," or "silk press," and discover stylists they haven't met yet.

Every photo in the feed leads back to the stylist who created it. When someone taps a look, they see the price, appointment length, portfolio, reviews from real completed appointments, and everything they need to decide whether to book.

This page exists for one reason: to help new clients find you.

You don't need a large Instagram following. You don't need someone to recommend you first. If your work is strong, it can be discovered by people nearby who are already looking for that service.

The more high quality work you upload, the more opportunities you have to appear when someone searches for the styles you offer. Your portfolio becomes your storefront, and every new client starts here.

Booking

Booking

Why deposits: every booking requires a deposit at the moment of booking, charged immediately, not chased afterward in a DM. If the stylist cancels, it refunds automatically; if the client no-shows, the stylist keeps it. Enforced deposits are documented to cut no-show rates significantly compared to platforms that leave it optional.
Why availability: the calendar only ever shows times the stylist has actually marked as open, enforced by the system, so a client can't request a time that isn't real and two clients can't be placed in the same slot.
Why pricing: the price a client sees and pays is the exact price the stylist set on their own profile — nothing renegotiated in messages.
Why clients choose services, not just "book me": the client picks the specific service she wants (with its real duration and price) before ever picking a time, so both sides know exactly what's being booked.
Why this removes friction: a confirmed booking exists as a real record the instant it's made — nothing about it depends on a reply, a follow-up text, or someone remembering to confirm.

Business

Running your business

This is the stylist side of the website — the dashboard, messages, calendar, portfolio, reviews, clients, and analytics, working together in one place. Bookings, Clients, Portfolio, and Home are the four sections the stylist toolkit is actually built around, in that priority order.
• Dashboard — today's earnings, upcoming bookings, and pending requests waiting on an Approve or Decline.
• Messages — every thread is pinned to the specific booking it belongs to, not a general inbox.
• Calendar — fills itself in the moment a booking is confirmed, no manual entry.
• Portfolio — the stylist's own uploaded work, the same photos that determine what shows up in Discovery.
• Reviews — can only attach to a completed, paid appointment, so every review is tied to a real client.
• Clients — every booked client is added automatically, building a real client list without manual data entry.
• Analytics — profile views, saves, where those views are coming from, and which of a stylist's own looks are performing best.
How they work together: one confirmed booking updates all of it at once — the calendar, the earnings total, the client list, and the message thread — without the stylist logging anything by hand.

The Money

One booking, dollar by dollar

Worked example on a $100 service, at the platform's default 25% deposit (a stylist can set their own deposit anywhere from 15% to 100%; this is the default):
Client pays a $25 deposit today.
Stylist keeps $18.97 from that deposit today.
Snooty's Booking Share is a flat $5.00 — 5% of the full $100 appointment value, collected out of that same $25 deposit, not billed separately.
Stripe's own card-processing fee is $1.03 (its standard 2.9% + $0.30); Snooty does not mark this up, and it comes out of the stylist's payout, not Snooty's share.
The remaining $75.00 balance is paid at the chair, same as it always would be.

Total to the stylist on this booking: $93.97.
Remember StyleSeat's and Booksy's fees from a few slides back — on this same $100 booking, either one would have taken several times what Snooty just did. And the $5.00 Booking Share holds flat no matter what deposit percentage a stylist sets, from 15% up to 100%.

The Money

The only other charge

The one other cost on Snooty is a $50 Annual Listing Fee — paid once a year, at onboarding, to go live.
Broken down small: that's about $0.14 a day. Less than a dollar a week. One completed booking at any price point pays it back several times over.
It is not a monthly subscription and it does not change based on how many bookings come in — unlike the competitor monthly fees named earlier, which bill every month whether or not the stylist gets booked that month.
After the $50/year is paid, Snooty only makes money when the stylist gets booked. No booking, no other charge.
